One Line Elevator Pitch

Most outbound tools help you build lists or send sequences - Unify is different. We start with signals - who's on your site, who's engaging with your content, who just hired, who's interacting with your brand on LinkedIn - and we turn those into executed outbound plays. Not a dashboard of insights. Not another list. Actual emails, actual sequences, actual pipeline. The fastest-growing companies in the world - Perplexity, Together AI, Juicebox - use Unify as the automated engine behind their whole outbound motion. Your team still owns the strategy. Unify runs it.
The 90-second version
Think about what your outbound motion actually costs you. You've got a RevOps person stitching together Clay, Apollo, an enrichment tool, a sequencer, a deliverability layer, slack alerts, and a CRM sync - eight tools, thousands of hours a quarter of maintenance, and it's still brittle. Unify collapses that into one system. You tell us what a good account looks like and what a good signal looks like - we do the rest. We build the list, enrich it, route it, write it, send it, warm up the mailboxes, protect the domains, and push every outcome into your CRM. The math our customers land on: one deal sourced from Unify pays for the whole year. Campfire doubled their pipeline. Perplexity generated $1.7M in 90 days. The reason isn't that we have better AI - it's that we're the only platform built signal-first, and we run managed deliverability as a service.
Three rules for every conversation
1. Lead with the elevator pitch. Everything flows from it.
2. Anchor to the customer's own words from discovery, onboarding, or the last QBR. Never show a generic pitch in a post-sales conversation.
3. Every conversation ends with a specific next step - a date, an outcome, and an owner. "I'll follow up" is how you lose renewals.

The two differentiators

Every competitive talk track collapses into one of these. Memorize both.

#1 Signal-first, not list-first

Clay, Apollo, ZoomInfo, Amplemarket - they all start with "who do you want to reach?" You build a list, then you try to figure out when to hit it. That's list-first. It's why those tools end up with open rates in the sub-40% range and why outbound feels like shouting into the void.

Unify starts with a different question: "who's showing us they're ready to buy right now?" Web intent, champion tracking, new hires, LinkedIn engagement, G2 visits, AI agent research, form drop-offs, newsletter clicks- we watch the signal surface we enrich the prospects, and execute the outbound because the signal fired.

Clay and Apollo are list-building tools with a sequencer bolted on. You build the list, then you hope something happens. Unify is the opposite- we watch for signals, and we build the list in response to the signal. That's why our customers see 3-5x the reply rates on signal-triggered plays compared to blast sequences. You're not doing more outbound, you're doing more relevant outbound.
List-first is fishing with a net. Signal-first is fishing with a sonar. Same water, same fish- dramatically different catch rate.
Where this is going
We're building a list builder, but it won't be another Clay clone. It'll give you the firmographic data you'd expect, and layer our signal taxonomy on top. A whole other dimension of list building: not just "who fits your ICP," but "who fits your ICP and is showing intent right now." On the roadmap.
#2 Managed Deliverability

Every other tool in the category dumps deliverability on the customer. Buy mailboxes, manage warmup, monitor reputation, rotate domains, watch for bounces, fix the SPF/DKIM, hope nothing catches fire. It's a second full-time job most customers never wanted.

Unify runs it as a service. Domains, mailboxes, warmup, rotation, reputation monitoring, throttling, list hygiene — we own the infrastructure. When RevOps looks at our line item, roughly half the perceived value is the deliverability work they don't have to do anymore.

The thing nobody talks about in this category is that half the reason outbound works or fails is deliverability. Clay doesn't touch it. No Sales Engagement Platform touches it. We run it as a service- domains, mailboxes, warmup, reputation, hygiene, all of it. When your RevOps team thinks about what they'd have to rebuild to leave us, this is usually the part that makes them stop.
The line item you're paying for Unify- roughly half of it is the deliverability layer. If you rebuild it yourself, you're hiring a half-FTE and buying four tools.

Save - Unhealthy Growth Team

Setup: Customer signed up for growth outbound. It's not working (or they don't feel like it is). Wavering on renewal. Job: Diagnose which of the 5 failure modes they're in, pick the matching save, re-anchor them to the mode they should have been in from the start.

The 5 failure modes

Failure A - They didn't set up plays▶

Logging into a platform with nothing running. Build one play live on the call, using their data, and commit to a weekly check-in for 30 days.

Let's not pretend the last quarter didn't happen- you haven't gotten value because the machine wasn't turned on. I'm going to build your first play right now, with your data, in your tenant. We'll agree on one signal and one audience, and I'll send you the logs every Monday morning until you're confident. We can't judge Unify until we actually run it- and we've never actually run it.
Failure B - Too much noise, can't prioritize▶

Tighten signal criteria. Keep them sending emails — alerts alone will churn.

You built for volume, and now you're drowning in Slack alerts and your team is ignoring them. Let's fix that in two moves. First, we kill every signal below [threshold] - tighten the criteria. Second, make sure the automated email motion is still running in the background on the broader TAM. Your team only sees the top 20 signals per week in Slack, the machine handles the rest via email. Nobody's drowning but the pipeline keeps building.
Failure C - Wrong person on our side▶

Champion left or never was the DM. Multi-threading save. Get to power fast.

I want to be direct with you - I don't think we're going to get a fair read on Unify without looping in [VP Sales / Head of RevOps / CMO]. Not because you're not making the call, but because the story of what's working and what's not is getting lost if it's only living with you. Can we set up 30 minutes with [name] so I can walk them through what we've found?
Failure D - Attribution problem▶

They're winning deals but not crediting Unify. Reporting/storytelling save - build the report yourself.

I want to show you something. I pulled every meeting booked in the last 90 days where Unify was involved at any touchpoint. [N] of those are now opportunities. [$] is in pipeline. [$] is closed-won. You may have been discounting the impact because nobody was doing this math. Let me send this to you every month so you can show your CEO.
Failure E - Genuine product gap▶

CRM, full LinkedIn automation, native dialer, etc. Don't pretend. Acknowledge, explain workaround/roadmap, anchor on what we do deliver.

You're right, [gap] is a real limitation. I'm not going to tell you it isn't. Here's what I can tell you: it's on our roadmap for [timeline], here's how we're working around it today, and here's what you'd lose if you left Unify to get [gap] from [competitor]. I want to make sure we're having the real tradeoff conversation, not just the feature-by-feature one.

The close

I want to leave this call with a specific 30-day plan we both agree on. If in 30 days the plan is executing, we have a good renewal conversation. If it isn't, I'll tell you straight, this isn't working and we should talk about a different path. Does that feel fair?

Redirect-Maintain — Healthy × Bridge

Setup: Customer is a Full Sales Team, SDRs, etc. with product friction. Happy, but not a growth story. Job: Keep them renewing at current levels. Reframe toward growth only if real upside exists.

You're getting what you came here for- [their actual use case]. That's good. Here's the honest question: is there a next use case we should be thinking about, or is this the right shape of our partnership for another year? I'd rather lock in what works than push you into something you don't need.

The reframe question (use sparingly)

Want me to walk through what that would look like for you? No pressure - I just want to make sure we're not leaving value on the table.

The maintenance close

Here's what I'd like to do. Lock the renewal now so we're not having this conversation in a crunch. Same terms, same package, same motion. If in Q2 or Q3 something changes and you want to expand, we'll talk about it then. Sound good?
Watch out
The Redirect-Maintain customer who looks healthy but isn't really using much of the platform. Low usage & narrow use case = slow-motion churn. If that's what you see, the account belongs in Triage.

Triage - Deprioritized Accounts

Setup: At risk, never got off the ground, not a core customer profile. Job: Diagnose fast. Save aggressively or exit cleanly.

Diagnostic questions - ask all three

  1. "What problem did you buy Unify to solve? Six months in, is that problem better, worse, or the same?"
  2. "If you weren't paying for Unify today, would you buy it again? What would you buy instead?"
  3. "Who inside your company is the loudest voice for keeping Unify? Who's the loudest voice for cutting it?"

If there IS a path

Pick a Save-Grow move and apply it aggressively. No reactivations.

If there ISN'T a path - the honest exit

I want to be straight with you. Based on what you're describing, I don't think forcing another year is the right thing for either of us. Let's end cleanly, I'll make sure your data exports cleanly and I'll write a handoff doc for whoever takes the use case next. And if 12-18 months from now your motion changes and you're running real growth outbound, I want to be your first call. Here's my number.
Why this matters
Clean exits turn into re-engagement deals 6-12 months later. Forced renewals turn into angry churns and G2 reviews. The team that saves everything at all costs is the team whose NDR looks good for one quarter and catastrophic for three.

Build vs Buy

With Cowork, Clay, and agentic workflows everywhere, "why wouldn't I just build this myself?" is coming up constantly. The most important objection to handle cleanly.

The diagnostic question
Walk me through what "build it yourself" looks like for you. What tools, who maintains it, and what happens when it breaks?
The three-layer framework
There are three layers to what Unify does: the signal layer, the execution layer, and the deliverability layer. You can absolutely build the first two yourself - Clay for lists, a sequencer for sends, some Zapier glue in between. A lot of teams do. The question is the third layer. Managed deliverability - domains, mailboxes, warmup, reputation, rotation, list hygiene - is the part nobody can build with duct tape. It's also the part that determines whether your emails land or bounce. And it's the part that breaks invisibly until your reply rates crater and you don't know why.
The maintenance tax angle
Building it is the fun part. Maintaining it is the job. The teams that build their own outbound stack spend 15-25 hours a week keeping it alive - that's a half-FTE of ops maintenance. That person is not building strategy. They're babysitting workflows. When they leave, the machine breaks. When a tool in the chain updates its API, the machine breaks. When your volume scales, the machine breaks. Unify is the answer to "what if the machine just worked?"
The cost angle
Let's add it up. Clay: $X. Instantly or Smartlead: $Y. RB2B or Clearbit: $Z. HeyReach for LinkedIn. A Zapier plan. Your ops person's time at $150K loaded. That's already approaching or exceeding what you'd pay for Unify - and you still don't have the signal layer, the play builder, the agent logic, or the managed deliverability. The build-it-yourself stack is only cheaper if you don't count the people.
The Cowork / AI-agent angle
I get it - AI agents can automate a lot of this now. But there's a difference between "I can automate the glue between tools" and "I have a platform that was built to orchestrate signals into outbound." The agent can stitch the API calls together. It can't manage your deliverability infrastructure. It can't optimize your play performance across 7,000 plays. It can't tell you which signal combination generates the highest reply rates. Automation is the commodity. Orchestration & infrastructure is the moat.
When to concede
If the team is <5 people, spending <$10K/year on outbound tools, and has a technical founder who enjoys building the stack, let them. They're not a Unify customer yet. Plant the seed: "When you hit the scale where maintaining the stack costs more than the stack itself, that's when we talk."

We don't offer opt-outs because the motion takes 60-90 days to hit its stride and any tool that lets you quit in 30 days is incentivized against you seeing value. "Your price is too high vs. [Apollo / Amplemarket / Clay-stack]"▶
Let's run the actual math. Pull up what you'd pay for [Apollo / Amplemarket + a dialer + a sequencer + deliverability]. Now add your ops person's time. Now add the deliverability risk cost. Now subtract the signal layer and the plays, because those tools don't have them. The price difference usually shrinks or reverses when we look at the real line-item comparison. I'll build the slide for you if you want to take it to your CFO.
"We're going to rebuild it ourselves in Clay"▶
I'd actually push back on the framing. Clay is great at list building- keep using it for that. What you'd be rebuilding is the signal layer, the execution layer, and the deliverability layer. That's not a Clay thing. A lot of teams try to build that in Clay + Instantly + HeyReach + glue, and here's what usually happens: the first 60 days are fine. Then the person maintaining it gets pulled to another project, a sequence breaks silently, nobody notices for two weeks, and suddenly you're not getting signals anymore. The real question isn't "Clay or Unify?", it's "Clay for lists + Unify for action, or Clay for everything and 20 hours a week of maintenance?"
"We have Outreach and we're not moving off it"▶
You shouldn't. We sit above Outreach, not instead of it. Your reps can live in Outreach while Unify runs the signal and deliverability layer that feeds it. The two products compose natively. Nobody has to switch tools to get the value.

The most dangerous objection in the book
Almost always means two things: a motion problem and a storytelling problem.
I hear that a lot, and I want to be honest about it. This is on us as much as it's on you. Let me book time minutes with your team next week and we'll do two things. Field rules (don'ts)

Don't lose a renewal on a stupid mistake. These come out of the closed-lost analysis.

Don't pitch features in a post-sales conversation. Every feature you pitch is a minute you're not talking about the customer's pipeline. Connect every feature to a specific outcome.

Don't accept "we're not really using it" without a plan. Diagnose on the spot, commit to a 30-day plan with a specific outcome and date.

Don't try to save a Triage account at all costs. Clean exits come back 12 months later. Forced renewals turn into angry churns and G2 reviews.

Don't defend the price - attack the total cost of the alternative. Reframe to 8 tools + ops time + deliverability risk. Never defend the sticker.

Don't let "follow up" be your close. Every call ends with a specific time, outcome, and owner. "Let's put 30 minutes on Thursday at 2pm - I'll have the usage pull ready, we'll decide together."

Don't let the roadmap be a secret. Customers who know what's coming renew. Every QBR includes the "what's shipping this quarter" slide - once Q2 planning locks.

Don't forget to multi-thread. If only your champion is in the room, you're coaching instead of selling. You need at least one conversation with the EB before the renewal meeting.

Don't make the customer tell the Unify story for you. Send the usage report, pipeline attribution, wins list every 30 days. If they have to build the case themselves, they won't.
